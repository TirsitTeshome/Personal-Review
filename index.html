Personal Reflection on Born a Crime Book by Trevor Noah

If I could give “Born a Crime” a new title, it would be “ From the Black, White and Beyond: Navigating a Divided World.”

While the existing title is undeniably impactful in drawing attention to the illegality of his birth, “From the Black, White and Beyond: navigating a divided world” better describes the reality of race in shaping Trevor’s early life. The phrases “black” and “white” literally evoke the racial classifications of apartheid. Metaphorically, “black and white” refers to the good and the bad, representing Trevor’s experience of both sides of the world. However “and beyond” is crucial because it speaks to his mixed race identity.

"Abel wanted a traditional marriage with a traditional wife but he never fell in love with a subservient woman, He only wants a woman who is free because his dream is to put her in a cage"

The quote is a strange and a very sad thought about how some people perceive most relationships. It shows how they want to control someone, in the name of love. This quote highlights how easily people fall into social aspects that value a woman that is “free and independent” until she has a husband. Then she has to be fully controlled. We see this happening today in many ways.

Think about a woman who has a good job, but everyone expects her to prioritize her husband's career over her own. Or a mom who stays home with her kids, but people judge her for not having her own career. It's the idea that a woman should be primarily defined by her relationship and her home life, not for who she is as a person.
From my own experience, I have seen this play out with female family members who were initially praised for being ground breakers in their careers, only to face pressure to conform to traditional roles once married. Their individuality became a source of tension and conflict. Abel’s desire for traditional marriage is not inherently wrong, the contradiction lies in wanting a free-spirited partner only to then suppress that spirit. This quote serves as a reminder of the importance of recognizing and challenging these paradoxical expectations that limit both men and women, and ultimately poison relationships.
